\section{Introduction}

This project examines income prediction and population segmentation using data from the U.S. Census Current Population Survey. The analysis addresses two complementary objectives: predicting whether an individual is a high-income earner and identifying meaningful subgroups within the population based on demographic, employment, and socioeconomic characteristics.

The first objective is formulated as a supervised classification problem. The goal is to predict whether an individual's income exceeds \$50,000 using demographic, employment, and socioeconomic features. In addition to predictive performance, emphasis is placed on model interpretability to identify the factors most strongly associated with income level.

The second objective is formulated as an unsupervised segmentation problem. Rather than predicting a predefined outcome, this analysis seeks to uncover natural groupings within the population. The resulting segments provide a descriptive view of population structure and can support the development of targeted marketing strategies, customer profiles, and resource allocation approaches.

Together, these approaches provide both individual-level predictions and group-level insights. Classification supports the identification of higher-income individuals, while segmentation provides a broader understanding of population heterogeneity. Combined, they offer a data-driven foundation for marketing and customer analysis.
